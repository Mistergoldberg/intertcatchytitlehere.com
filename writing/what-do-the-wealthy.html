What Do The Wealthy, The Sun, and Popular Kids Have in Common?

🧭 Let’s Get Metaphysical

If you want to understand systems—economic, social, cultural—you eventually have to get a little metaphysical.

Not in a robe-and-incense way.

In a first-principles way.

🧠 Metaphysics, Not Incense

Metaphysics is the branch of philosophy concerned with the underlying structure of reality: being, knowing, cause, identity, time, and space.

It’s what you reach for when surface explanations stop working.

When “people are greedy” or “that’s just how it is” no longer explains the pattern, metaphysics asks a more useful question:

What kind of system produces this outcome?

🎣 Start With a Riddle

What do the wealthy, the sun, and popular kids have in common—and why should you care?

They attract things.

More precisely: they attract and accumulate what the system values.

  • The sun has accumulated the most matter in the solar system
  • The wealthy have accumulated the most money in the economy
  • Popular kids have accumulated the most social connections in a school

Different domains.

Same pattern.

This is the 80/20 rule in action: roughly 20% of nodes in a network account for 80% of outcomes.

It’s not a moral judgment.

It’s a distribution pattern—and once you see it, you start seeing it everywhere.

🕸️ Network Science

The U.S. National Research Council defines network science as the study of network representations of physical, biological, and social phenomena.

That sounds complicated. It isn’t.

At the core, networked systems follow a few simple rules.

Rule one: nodes compete to collect points.

Money. Matter. Attention. Status. Calories. Likes.

Whatever the system rewards.

This isn’t a human invention. It’s older than us.

Collecting points appears to be one of the universe’s favorite algorithms.

Rule two: growth feeds itself.

Once a node accumulates slightly more than its neighbors, it gains an advantage. That advantage creates a feedback loop—what scientists call an autocatalytic reaction—where each gain makes the next gain easier.

Think compound interest. Think viral spread. Think popularity.

Two becomes four. Four becomes eight.

Not because anyone planned it—but because the structure rewards early accumulation.

Rule three: distribution is uneven.

A small subset of nodes becomes disproportionately large. Most do not.

That’s the 80/20 rule again.

☀️ The Solar System Is a Network

To see this cycle in its purest form, look up.

In the early solar system, matter was unevenly distributed. One region accumulated slightly more mass. That mass increased gravitational pull. Increased pull attracted more matter.

Feedback kicked in.

The result?

The sun now holds 99.8% of the matter in the entire solar system.

Once it crossed a threshold, everything else became orbit.

This is what attraction looks like when stripped of morality.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 Sociograms and Schoolyards

Social networks behave the same way.

Psychiatrist Jacob Moreno visualized social structures using sociograms—diagrams where people are nodes and relationships are connections.

Nodes with more connections appear larger. Nodes with fewer appear smaller.

The shapes are unmistakable. They mimic the solar system.

A few large nodes dominate the network. Many smaller nodes orbit them. Some barely connect at all.

Macro or micro. Physical or social. Matter or meaning. Same geometry.

⚖️ Why This Matters More Than You Think

Historian Walter Scheidel, in The Great Leveler: Violence and the History of Inequality from the Stone Age to the Twenty-First Century, showed that this pattern predates markets, capitalism, and written law.

In prehistoric grave sites, archaeologists found that roughly 20% of the dead were buried with about 80% of the valuables.

The pattern was already there.

Which tells us something uncomfortable:

  • Inequality isn’t just cultural or political.
  • It’s structural.
  • It emerges naturally in networked systems unless something actively disrupts it.

This doesn’t tell us what should happen.

It tells us what does happen.

Understanding that difference is the beginning of agency.

❓ So Why Should You Care?

Because these same dynamics shape:

  • Who gets heard
  • Who gets funded
  • Who gets promoted
  • Who gets ignored
  • Who gets stuck

They explain why some ideas compound while others vanish.

Why some people seem to live inside gravity wells of opportunity.

Why bad luck clusters—and so does good luck.

They don’t explain everything.

But they explain more than we’re comfortable admitting.

That’s what this series of essays is all about.
