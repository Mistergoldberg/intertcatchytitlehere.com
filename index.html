I Design Systems.
Then I Stress Them Until They Reveal Where Value Actually Moves.

Art. Retail. Media. Food. Education.
If it’s a system, I’m interested.

Welcome. I’m Jared.

I’ve been unusually lucky.

Not in the lottery sense — but in where I’ve landed inside systems while they were under pressure.

I grew up inside a family wholesale business, learning early that margins, expectations, and logistics matter more than good intentions. As a kid, that meant bottling glitter into private-label jars and discovering, very practically, that customers don’t buy what you mean — they buy what works.

That curiosity carried me to China, where I spent a decade inside large-scale manufacturing, product development, and global retail. I worked on factory floors, ran human-factor research, built products for mass markets, and learned how ideas survive — or die — once they meet tooling, timelines, and buyers with leverage.

Later, I moved into North American retail and platforms, working inside organizations where supply chains, merchandising, data, media, and incentives collide. Different scale. Same underlying dynamics.

Across all of it, one pattern kept repeating:

Most value isn’t created by ideas.
It’s created by structure.

Who decides.
What gets measured.
Where feedback loops close — or don’t.

That’s the work I do now:
finding leverage inside messy reality, and explaining it clearly enough that other people can use it.

I like complexity, human behavior, and the 20% that quietly drives the other 80%.
I use humor because it’s efficient — not because things are funny.

I make art. I cook obsessively. I write essays. I run workshops.
Different outputs. Same obsession.

People who tend to like Marshall McLuhan, Marcel Duchamp, George Carlin, Jonathan Swift, Voltaire, Plato, or Jeff Koons usually feel at home here.
