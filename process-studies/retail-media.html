Retail Media

Turning store screens into measurable retail infrastructure.

🧠 Why This Mattered

Retail media sounds simple from a distance. Put screens in stores. Sell inventory. Measure performance. But that is mostly fantasy written by people who do not have to live with store operations.

Inside the real system, media surfaces sit in tension with merchandising, dealer or store realities, labor, hardware reliability, customer flow, privacy constraints, and the quiet politics of ownership. The mess is not a side effect. The mess is the work.

What interested me was not “ads on screens.” It was the deeper coordination problem: how to turn scattered media touchpoints into infrastructure that operators can trust, merchants can understand, brands can buy, and leadership can measure without lying to itself.

2,400 stores and environments that force every design choice to survive contact with operations
20,000+ endpoints and surfaces once the ecosystem is treated as infrastructure instead of a campaign
1 shared language for merchants, operators, media teams, and measurement logic
0 interest in vanity dashboards that make everyone feel busy without clarifying ownership
🧪 Study Folds
Context

In-store media sits inside one of the most structurally awkward zones in retail. Everyone wants the upside. Few teams want the operating burden. Screens are visible, but the real system is invisible: who owns content logic, who handles failure, how surfaces are prioritized, and what counts as success when stores are not websites.

The system problem

Media, store ops, merchandising, and measurement do not naturally align. They optimize for different outcomes and move at different speeds. One team wants sell-through. Another wants uptime. Another wants brand revenue. Another wants clean attribution. Without a shared logic, the platform becomes a polite pile of contradictions.

The core problem was not a lack of screens. It was a lack of coherent system ownership.
If incentives are misaligned, every “platform” becomes a dressed-up negotiation.
My approach

I start by defining surfaces, incentives, and owners. What exactly exists? Who is responsible when it underperforms? What does each stakeholder believe they are buying or protecting? Once those questions are made explicit, the roadmap gets much less mystical.

The practical work was to unify language across teams, reduce ambiguity in surface definitions, expose the dependencies hidden inside “simple” requests, and translate non-linear system behavior into structures a larger organization could execute against.

What changed

Once the system is named properly, the roadmap changes shape. Conversations move from “Can we launch this?” to “Who absorbs the cost of operating this, and how does it scale?” That shift alone improves decisions.

What changes is not just capability. It is clarity. Better planning logic. Better sequencing. Better definition of what is truly measurable. Better conditions for self-serve and operational reliability.

Clearer ownership across media, stores, merchandising, and platform teams
Tighter roadmap logic around dependencies instead of feature wishlists
Better measurement conversations grounded in physical reality, not borrowed ecommerce language
More usable ordering and planning workflows for the people who actually have to run them
Screens / artifacts

The most useful artifacts in this kind of work are rarely glamorous. They are the diagrams and frameworks that stop teams from talking past one another: ecosystem maps, workflow states, screen taxonomies, ownership models, and ordering logic.

What I’d do differently

I would model operating burden earlier and more aggressively. Teams usually underestimate the downstream cost of maintaining a “flexible” media system. Flexibility without ownership becomes debt quickly.

I would also push even harder for shared definitions before dashboarding. Measurement gets political fast. If the terms are loose, the numbers will eventually be used as decoration.
